# Plain-Language Checklist for Client and Stakeholder Communications

This checklist distills the U.S. federal government's Plain Language
Guidelines (plainlanguage.gov, public domain / CC0) into a form usable for
legal-team communications. It gives a reviewer a fast set of prose checks for
making a client update, business memo cover note, or stakeholder-facing
summary easier for a non-lawyer to read and act on.

## Boundary — read this before applying the checklist

**This checklist applies only to client-facing and business-stakeholder
communication** — cover emails, plain-language summaries, business-stakeholder
summary sections (see `core/business-stakeholder-communication.md`), and
similar audience-facing prose. It does **not** apply to legal drafting itself:
contract clauses, statutory or regulatory analysis, citations, defined terms,
pleadings, or any text where precision, defined meaning, or established legal
phrasing must outrank readability. Simplifying a contract clause or a legal
conclusion for plainness can silently change its legal effect. Where a skill's
Output Format calls for a **Business Stakeholder Summary** or similar plain
layer sitting alongside the full legal deliverable, apply this checklist to
that layer only — never to the underlying legal analysis, quotations, or
clause language it summarizes.

## The checklist

- **Keep sentences short.** One main idea per sentence. *Instead of:* "In the
  event that the counterparty fails to cure the breach within the notice
  period specified above, the agreement may thereupon be terminated." *Write:*
  "If the other side doesn't fix the problem within 30 days, we can end the
  contract."

- **Use active voice; name who acts.** *Instead of:* "The notice must be sent
  by the disclosing party." *Write:* "You must send the notice."

- **Use common words over legal or Latinate ones.** *Instead of:* "prior to,"
  "in the event that," "pursuant to." *Write:* "before," "if," "under."

- **Avoid double negatives.** *Instead of:* "Nothing herein shall be deemed to
  prevent a party from not renewing." *Write:* "Either party may choose not to
  renew."

- **Front-load the main point.** Put the bottom line in the first sentence,
  not the last. *Instead of:* four sentences of background before "so we
  recommend you sign," *write:* "We recommend signing, for these reasons: ..."

- **Define a term once, then use it consistently.** *Instead of:* switching
  between "the Vendor," "the Company," and "they" for the same party mid-memo,
  *write:* pick one label after the first mention and keep it throughout.

- **Use "must" for obligations, "may" for options — and say which.**
  *Instead of:* "The parties should consider providing notice," *write:* "You
  must give 30 days' notice" or "You may give notice at any time" — whichever
  the source document actually says.

- **Use lists and tables for parallel items.** *Instead of:* a paragraph
  burying five conditions in prose, *write:* a short bulleted or tabular list
  of the five conditions.

- **Address the reader directly.** *Instead of:* "The recipient of this
  memorandum is advised that...", *write:* "You should know that..."

- **State the decision, not just the analysis.** *Instead of:* ending with
  only a description of the issue, *write:* a plain statement of what decision
  is actually on the table and who owns it (see `core/business-stakeholder-communication.md`'s "Decision Needed").

- **Cut throat-clearing and filler.** *Instead of:* "It is important to note
  that, moving forward, careful consideration should be given to...", *write:*
  the point itself, directly.

- **Explain a term of art the first time it appears, briefly.** *Instead of:*
  using "indemnification" unexplained, *write:* "indemnification — one side
  covering the other's losses if a claim comes in."

- **Keep numbers and dates concrete, and flag any unverified one.** *Instead
  of:* "shortly before the deadline," *write:* "by [DATE — from the notice],"
  and use `[deadline verification required]` where the date has not been
  independently confirmed. Plain language never means guessing a number to
  make a sentence read more smoothly.

## What this checklist does not do

- It does not replace `skills/legal-methodology/legal-prose-polish/SKILL.md`,
  which handles AI-sounding filler, hedging, and legal-writing quality more
  broadly and applies to attorney-facing drafts as well.
- It does not authorize simplifying a legal conclusion, a risk rating, or a
  citation. Placeholders (`[CONFIRM: ...]`, `[verify jurisdiction]`) must
  remain visible in a plain-language pass exactly as they would in the full
  legal draft.
- It is a style checklist, not a substitute for attorney review. Every
  plain-language deliverable is still draft legal work product for attorney
  review, not legal advice.
