# Severance and Release Red-Flag Catalog

This catalog lists clause patterns and structural features that a reviewer should actively scan for during a severance or separation agreement review. Each entry describes the pattern, why it matters, what to check, and which party is most exposed. The catalog covers both perspectives: risk to the departing employee from employer-drafted terms, and enforceability or compliance exposure to the employer from defective drafting. It is organized by clause category to support a Red Flags Quick Scan output within the employment skills.

This catalog is a review aid. It identifies what to look for and why. Whether a flagged pattern is actually problematic depends on the specific agreement language, the reviewing party's role, the jurisdiction, the termination context, and attorney judgment. Every flagged item is a candidate for attorney verification — not a legal conclusion. Nothing here asserts what is or is not waivable, enforceable, or required in any jurisdiction.

---

## How to Use This Catalog

For each clause category below:

1. Locate the relevant provision in the agreement under review (or note its absence — a missing provision is itself a red flag in many contexts).
2. Check the pattern description against the actual language.
3. If the pattern is present, add the item to the issues table and the Red Flags Quick Scan.
4. Note which party is most exposed and whether the reviewing party matches the exposure profile.
5. Flag every finding for attorney review; do not resolve ambiguity silently, and never assert enforceability or waivability conclusions.

Each section closes with a **Preferred position** and **Fallback position** line — direction for counsel to weigh when negotiating, never final language and never a substitute for attorney judgment.

## 1. Release Scope

### 1.1 Release purporting to cover claims some jurisdictions do not permit waiving

**Pattern:** The release is drafted as a waiver of "any and all claims" without carve-outs for categories of claims that some jurisdictions do not permit an employee to waive by private agreement — for example, certain wage claims, workers' compensation claims, unemployment benefits, or vested benefit entitlements `[verify jurisdiction]`.

**Why it matters:** For the employee, an overbroad release creates confusion about what rights actually survive and can chill legitimate claims. For the employer, releasing non-waivable claims can render the provision — or in some regimes the release as a whole — vulnerable to challenge `[verify jurisdiction]`.

**What to check:**
- Quote the release language verbatim, including the list of released claim categories.
- List every express carve-out present; note the absence of carve-outs for categories commonly treated as non-waivable `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM: which claims are non-waivable in the governing jurisdiction]`.
- Check for a severability clause addressing what happens if part of the release fails.

**Most exposed:** Employee (chilled rights, uncertainty); employer (enforceability and compliance exposure).

---

### 1.2 Release of future claims or claims arising after signing

**Pattern:** The release covers claims "known or unknown, now existing or hereafter arising," or otherwise reaches conduct or claims that arise after the signing date.

**Why it matters:** A release of claims that have not yet arisen is a pattern many regimes treat with skepticism or refuse to enforce `[verify jurisdiction]`. For the employee it purports to strip rights for future employer conduct; for the employer it invites a challenge to the release.

**What to check:**
- Identify the temporal cut-off of the release: does it run through the signing date, the separation date, or indefinitely?
- Quote any "hereafter arising" or forward-looking language verbatim.
- If a second release or reaffirmation is contemplated for post-signing service, confirm it is separately documented and separately supported by consideration `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.

**Most exposed:** Employee (loss of future rights); employer (enforceability exposure).

---

### 1.3 No carve-out for agency-charge filing, cooperation, or participation rights

**Pattern:** The release and related covenants contain no language preserving the employee's right to file a charge or complaint with, communicate with, cooperate with, or participate in an investigation or proceeding of a government agency or regulator.

**Why it matters:** Some regimes protect these rights regardless of contract language and treat provisions purporting to waive them as unenforceable or as independent compliance violations `[Verify current law]`. Absence of the carve-out exposes the employer to regulatory risk and misleads the employee about surviving rights.

**What to check:**
- Search the entire agreement — release, confidentiality, non-disparagement, and cooperation sections — for agency-communication carve-outs, not just the release section.
- Note whether the carve-out, if present, also addresses monetary recovery from agency proceedings (e.g., whether the employee waives recovery but not participation) `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM: permissibility of a monetary-recovery waiver]`.
- Flag any language requiring the employee to notify the employer before communicating with a regulator.

**Most exposed:** Employer (regulatory and enforceability exposure); employee (chilled protected activity).

---

### 1.4 Released-parties definition broader than the consideration supports

**Pattern:** The "Released Parties" definition sweeps in affiliates, predecessors, successors, benefit plans, insurers, and individual officers and employees, while the consideration flows only from the direct employer entity.

**Why it matters:** The employee may be releasing claims against parties who provided nothing in exchange, including claims unrelated to the employment (for example, against an affiliate in a different capacity).

**What to check:**
- Quote the Released Parties definition and list each category of released person or entity.
- Check whether the employee holds any known claims or relationships (investor, customer, separate contract) with any released party beyond the employment.
- Note whether the release is mutual — does anyone release the employee?

**Most exposed:** Employee.

**Preferred position:** A release limited to claims arising from the employment relationship through the signing date, with express carve-outs for non-waivable claims `[verify jurisdiction]`, agency-charge filing and cooperation rights, vested benefits, and post-signing conduct.
**Fallback position:** A broad general release retained, but with a written carve-out schedule enumerating preserved rights and a severability clause, each carve-out confirmed by counsel against the governing jurisdiction `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.

## 2. Age-Discrimination Waiver Formalities

### 2.1 Missing or defective consideration-period and revocation-period recitations

**Pattern:** The agreement waives age-discrimination claims but omits, shortens, or muddles the jurisdiction- or statute-specific formalities that such waivers may require — for example, a stated period to consider the agreement, a stated period to revoke after signing, advice in writing to consult an attorney, and reference to the specific statute being waived `[Verify current law]`. This catalog does not assert what the required periods are; it flags their presence, absence, or internal inconsistency.

**Why it matters:** Where a statute conditions the validity of an age-discrimination waiver on specific formalities, a defective recitation can leave the waiver ineffective even though the employer paid full consideration — the employer's core exposure. For the employee, pressure to sign before a stated period ends is itself a flag (see Section 10).

**What to check:**
- Record verbatim every stated consideration period, revocation period, and effective-date mechanic. Do not compute or validate the day counts — `[deadline verification required]`; `[Verify current law]` for what the applicable statute requires.
- Check that the effective date is defined as after any revocation period expires, and that payment is not due before effectiveness.
- Confirm the agreement advises consulting an attorney and identifies the claims being waived `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM: required elements in the governing jurisdiction]`.
- Confirm employee age is known or flagged; if age 40+ status is unknown, flag it.

**Most exposed:** Employer (waiver invalidity while consideration is paid); employee (signing under defective process).

---

### 2.2 Group termination without required disclosure attachment

**Pattern:** The termination appears to be part of a group program (reduction in force, exit-incentive program, layoff wave), but the agreement includes no disclosure attachment describing the decisional unit, eligibility factors, and the job titles and ages of individuals selected and not selected — or the attachment is referenced but not provided `[Verify current law]`.

**Why it matters:** Some statutes condition group-termination waivers on specific written disclosures; a missing or defective disclosure can invalidate the waiver across the group — a multiplied employer exposure. A referenced-but-missing attachment is also a missing-document flag for any reviewer.

**What to check:**
- Determine from context whether the termination is part of a group program; if unknown, flag `[CONFIRM: whether this is a group termination]`.
- If a disclosure schedule is referenced, confirm it is attached and complete; if absent, flag as a missing document.
- Note any mismatch between the recitations (individual vs. group language) and the apparent facts.

**Most exposed:** Employer (program-wide waiver invalidity); employee (uninformed waiver decision).

**Preferred position:** Full statutory-formality recitations and, for any group program, a complete disclosure attachment — each element confirmed by counsel against current law `[Verify current law]` before the agreement is presented or signed.
**Fallback position:** If any formality element is uncertain, delay presentation or signing until counsel confirms the required elements; do not paper over a missing disclosure with a general acknowledgment clause `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.

## 3. Consideration Structure

### 3.1 Severance conditioned on obligations beyond the release

**Pattern:** Payment of severance is conditioned not only on signing (and not revoking) the release, but also on ongoing compliance with restrictive covenants, cooperation duties, transition services, or the employer's subjective satisfaction — so the "consideration" is really payment for continuing performance.

**Why it matters:** For the employee, conditioned payments can be withheld long after signing based on alleged breaches. For the employer, stacking conditions can blur what consideration supports the release and invite disputes over whether payment obligations were excused.

**What to check:**
- List every condition on each payment or benefit, and which conditions survive signing.
- Identify who decides whether a condition is satisfied and by what standard.
- Check whether a minor or immaterial alleged breach forfeits all remaining severance.

**Most exposed:** Employee.

---

### 3.2 Vague or discretionary clawback triggers

**Pattern:** The employer may claw back paid severance (or stop future installments) on triggers that are vague, subjective, or discretionary — "any breach," "conduct detrimental to the company," "as determined by the company in its sole discretion."

**Why it matters:** A discretionary clawback converts a fixed severance promise into an at-will payment and gives the employer post-signing leverage over the employee's conduct, references, and future employment choices.

**What to check:**
- Quote each clawback or cessation trigger verbatim.
- Is there a materiality threshold, a notice-and-cure mechanism, and an objective standard?
- Does clawback reach amounts already paid, and does the release remain effective even if severance is clawed back?

**Most exposed:** Employee.

---

### 3.3 Offset rights against wages, accrued leave, or other amounts already owed

**Pattern:** The agreement offsets the severance against — or conditions the release on — amounts the employer already owes: final wages, accrued but unused paid time off, earned commissions or bonuses, expense reimbursements, or vested benefits.

**Why it matters:** Amounts already owed are generally framed as items an employer cannot condition on signing a release — the employee is entitled to them regardless `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM: treatment of final wages and accrued leave in the governing jurisdiction]`. Structuring them as "consideration" can undermine the release (no new consideration) and create wage-payment compliance exposure for the employer.

**What to check:**
- Separate the stated consideration into (a) amounts already owed and (b) genuinely new consideration; flag any release supported only by (a).
- Check whether final-pay timing recited in the agreement is flagged, not validated — `[deadline verification required]` for any pay-timing rules.
- Note any general right of setoff against severance for disputed debts to the employer.

**Most exposed:** Employee (withheld entitlements); employer (wage-compliance and consideration-failure exposure).

**Preferred position:** Severance consisting of clearly new consideration, paid on a fixed schedule, conditioned only on a non-revoked release, with amounts already owed paid unconditionally and separately `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.
**Fallback position:** Conditions beyond the release limited to material, objectively defined breaches with notice and cure; clawback limited to prospective installments; already-owed amounts expressly excluded from conditions and offsets.

## 4. Restrictive Covenants

### 4.1 New or expanded non-compete or non-solicit introduced in the severance

**Pattern:** The severance agreement introduces a non-compete or non-solicit that did not exist during employment, or expands an existing covenant's duration, geography, or restricted activities.

**Why it matters:** The employee trades severance for new restrictions on future work — often the most valuable thing given up. For the employer, imposing new covenants at exit raises enforceability questions that vary sharply by jurisdiction, including regimes that restrict or prohibit such covenants entirely `[verify jurisdiction]`. This catalog flags the pattern; it does not assert enforceability.

**What to check:**
- Compare each covenant against the prior employment agreement or offer letter; mark each as "carried forward," "expanded," or "new." If prior agreements are not provided, flag them as missing documents.
- Record scope: restricted activities, geography, duration, and covered parties (customers, prospects, employees, contractors).
- Note whether the covenant applies regardless of the reason for termination.

**Most exposed:** Employee (future employability); employer (enforceability exposure) `[verify jurisdiction]`.

---

### 4.2 Covenants surviving beyond what prior agreements imposed, or unsupported by additional consideration

**Pattern:** The agreement recites that prior covenants "survive and are incorporated," but extends their running period (e.g., restarting the clock from the separation date), or imposes new or expanded covenants without identifying any consideration attributable to them beyond the severance that already supports the release.

**Why it matters:** Some regimes require separate or additional consideration for restrictive covenants, particularly ones imposed at or after termination `[verify jurisdiction]`. Silent extension of a covenant's clock is easy to miss and materially changes the employee's position.

**What to check:**
- Identify the start and end dates of each covenant period as written; record them without computing — `[deadline verification required]` where a date matters.
- Check whether any consideration is expressly allocated to the covenants.
- Check for a "tolling on breach" clause that can extend the covenant indefinitely.

**Most exposed:** Employee; employer (consideration-based enforceability exposure) `[verify jurisdiction]`.

**Preferred position:** No new or expanded covenants at exit; prior covenants carried forward unchanged, with their existence and terms confirmed against the actual prior agreements `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.
**Fallback position:** Any new restriction narrowed to identified customers or confidential-information misuse, time-limited, expressly supported by identified additional consideration, and confirmed by counsel against the governing jurisdiction's covenant rules `[verify jurisdiction]`.

## 5. Confidentiality and Non-Disparagement

### 5.1 Gag language reaching protected activity

**Pattern:** Confidentiality or non-disparagement obligations are broad enough to cover communications with regulators or government agencies, whistleblowing, truthful testimony under subpoena or in legal proceedings, participation in investigations, or discussion of working conditions with other workers — with no protected-activity carve-out.

**Why it matters:** Some regimes protect these activities notwithstanding contract language and treat provisions restricting them as unenforceable or independently unlawful `[Verify current law]`. The employer takes on compliance exposure; the employee's protected rights are chilled even if the clause would ultimately fail.

**What to check:**
- Read the confidentiality, non-disparagement, cooperation, and release sections together — a carve-out in one section may not cover obligations in another.
- List which protected activities are expressly carved out (regulator communications, testimony, whistleblower programs, concerted activity) and which are not `[Verify current law]`.
- Flag any clause requiring advance notice to, or consent from, the employer before lawful disclosures.

**Most exposed:** Employer (compliance and enforceability exposure); employee (chilled rights).

---

### 5.2 One-way non-disparagement

**Pattern:** The employee is barred from disparaging the employer, its products, and its personnel, with no reciprocal obligation on the employer — or with a reciprocal obligation limited to a handful of named officers "upon inquiry."

**Why it matters:** The employee's future employment prospects can be damaged by employer statements the agreement leaves unrestricted, while the employee bears clawback or damages exposure for any critical remark.

**What to check:**
- Is the obligation mutual? If nominally mutual, who is actually bound on the employer side (the entity cannot "speak" — is it limited to directors and officers, or anyone)?
- What is the standard — "disparage" undefined, or limited to false or misleading statements?
- What remedies attach to breach (clawback, liquidated damages, fees), and are they symmetric?

**Most exposed:** Employee.

---

### 5.3 Confidentiality of the agreement's existence or terms

**Pattern:** The agreement requires the employee to keep confidential not only trade secrets but the agreement's existence, its terms, or the underlying facts of the separation — sometimes including the facts of any alleged discrimination, harassment, or other workplace conduct.

**Why it matters:** Some regimes restrict or condition confidentiality of settlement terms or of underlying-conduct facts, particularly for certain claim types `[Verify current law]`. The employee may also be barred from disclosures needed for tax, legal, or financial advice, or to a spouse.

**What to check:**
- Identify exactly what must be kept confidential: terms, amount, existence, underlying facts.
- List the disclosure exceptions present (attorneys, tax advisors, immediate family, as required by law) and absent.
- Flag any restriction reaching the facts of alleged unlawful conduct `[Verify current law]`.

**Most exposed:** Employee; employer (enforceability and compliance exposure in restricting regimes) `[Verify current law]`.

**Preferred position:** Mutual non-disparagement limited to false or misleading statements, express protected-activity and advisor carve-outs in every restrictive section, and confidentiality limited to genuinely confidential business information `[Verify current law]`.
**Fallback position:** One-way obligations retained only with a defined disparagement standard, a full protected-activity carve-out, remedies limited to actual damages, and counsel confirmation that any terms-confidentiality clause is permitted for this claim mix `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.

## 6. Cooperation Obligations

### 6.1 Unlimited post-termination cooperation without compensation or time bounds

**Pattern:** The employee must cooperate with the employer in litigation, investigations, audits, or transitions — "as the company may reasonably request" — with no end date, no hourly or per-day compensation, no expense reimbursement, and no accommodation for the employee's new job.

**Why it matters:** An open-ended cooperation duty is an unpaid, indefinite service obligation that can conflict with the employee's next employer and can be used as a breach lever against remaining severance installments.

**What to check:**
- Duration: is the obligation time-limited or perpetual?
- Compensation and expenses: is the employee paid for time and reimbursed for costs? At what rate, and who decides?
- Interaction with truthful testimony: does the clause purport to shape the content of testimony rather than logistics? Flag any such language prominently `[Verify current law]`.
- Is cooperation a condition of severance (see Section 3.1)?

**Most exposed:** Employee.

**Preferred position:** Cooperation limited to a stated period and to matters arising from the employee's tenure, with reasonable notice, scheduling deference to new employment, expense reimbursement, and compensation for significant time — and no linkage to severance conditions.
**Fallback position:** An uncapped duty accepted only with expense reimbursement, a reasonableness standard, an express truthful-testimony carve-out, and breach consequences limited to material, uncured failures `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.

## 7. Benefits and Equity

### 7.1 Equity treatment inconsistent with, or unmoored from, plan documents

**Pattern:** The agreement recites equity treatment (vesting cessation, forfeiture, exercise windows, repurchase rights) that conflicts with — or cannot be verified against — the underlying plan and award documents, which are referenced but not provided.

**Why it matters:** Equity is often the largest economic item in a separation. A recitation that quietly forfeits awards the plan would preserve, shortens an exercise window, or adds a repurchase right changes real value; and where the agreement and plan conflict, which controls is a legal question for counsel.

**What to check:**
- List every equity award mentioned and every plan or award agreement referenced; flag each referenced-but-missing document as a missing document.
- Record recited vesting status, forfeiture triggers, and any exercise window verbatim — `[deadline verification required]` for any exercise or election window; do not compute it.
- Check for an integration or "controlling document" clause stating whether the agreement or the plan governs on conflict `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.

**Most exposed:** Employee.

---

### 7.2 Benefits-continuation ambiguity

**Pattern:** The agreement promises "continued benefits" or employer-subsidized coverage for a period, without specifying which plans, who pays what, how continuation interacts with statutory continuation rights, what happens if the employee becomes eligible elsewhere, or what happens if the plan terminates `[Verify current law]`.

**Why it matters:** Vague benefits promises produce post-signing disputes over the single item departing employees rely on most. For the employer, promises inconsistent with plan terms or continuation-coverage rules create plan-administration exposure.

**What to check:**
- Identify each promised benefit, the payer, the duration as stated (recorded, not computed — `[deadline verification required]`), and the termination triggers.
- Check whether the promise is framed as employer subsidy of statutory continuation coverage or as continued plan participation — and whether the plan permits the latter `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.
- Flag missing plan documents.

**Most exposed:** Employee (relied-upon coverage); employer (plan-administration exposure).

**Preferred position:** Equity and benefits treatment stated specifically, cross-checked against the actual plan documents (obtained before signing), with the more protective document controlling on conflict `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.
**Fallback position:** If plan documents cannot be obtained pre-signing, a representation from the employer that the recitations match the plans, plus a clause that plan terms control only where more favorable to the employee — flagged for counsel `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.

## 8. References and Rehire

### 8.1 No agreed reference language or reference procedure

**Pattern:** The agreement is silent on employment references, or leaves references to the employer's "standard policy," with no agreed statement of dates, title, and (if desired) reason for departure, and no designated contact point.

**Why it matters:** The employee gives up claims and accepts covenants but receives no protection on the narrative that most affects their next role. Silence also leaves individual managers free to characterize the departure inconsistently with the agreement's own recitals.

**What to check:**
- Is there an agreed reference letter or script, a neutral-reference commitment, or at least a designated respondent?
- Does any non-disparagement obligation on the employer (Section 5.2) practically cover reference responses?
- Is the internally/externally stated reason for departure consistent with the agreement's recitals?

**Most exposed:** Employee.

---

### 8.2 Blanket no-rehire covering affiliates

**Pattern:** The employee agrees never to apply for or accept employment with the employer — and the bar extends to all affiliates, successors, acquirers, or clients, sometimes with automatic-termination language if the employee is later found working for a covered entity.

**Why it matters:** A blanket no-rehire reaching a large corporate family can function like a non-compete, especially in concentrated industries or after future acquisitions the employee cannot foresee. Some regimes restrict no-rehire provisions in certain settlement contexts `[verify jurisdiction]`.

**What to check:**
- Which entities are covered, and does coverage float with future acquisitions?
- Is the bar time-limited or perpetual? Record the period; do not compute anything from it.
- What is the consequence if the employee inadvertently joins a covered entity (e.g., an acquirer of the new employer)?

**Most exposed:** Employee; employer (enforceability exposure in restricting regimes) `[verify jurisdiction]`.

**Preferred position:** Agreed reference language and a designated reference contact; no-rehire limited to the employing entity, time-limited, with an inadvertence/acquisition carve-out.
**Fallback position:** A neutral-reference (dates and title) commitment at minimum; any affiliate-wide no-rehire flagged for counsel with the covered-entity list narrowed and a carve-out for entities that become affiliates after signing `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.

## 9. Tax Treatment Hooks

This catalog never computes or asserts tax treatment. Every pattern in this section is an issue-spotting hook to route to `skills/tax/tax-issue-intake/SKILL.md` and to a qualified tax professional.

### 9.1 Characterization ambiguity in the payment allocation

**Pattern:** The agreement allocates the payment among categories (wages, non-wage damages, attorneys' fees, covenant consideration) with no stated basis, allocates nothing at all, or recites a characterization that appears driven by withholding convenience rather than the underlying claims.

**Why it matters:** Characterization affects withholding, reporting, and the parties' respective tax exposure — all matters outside this skill's competence. An unexplained allocation is a flag for tax review, not a conclusion.

**What to check:**
- Record the allocation verbatim, including amounts and reporting mechanics (payroll vs. other reporting) as stated.
- Note any indemnity making the employee responsible for the employer's withholding position.
- Route the allocation to `skills/tax/tax-issue-intake/SKILL.md`; flag for a tax professional. Do not opine on correct treatment.

**Most exposed:** Both parties (tax exposure runs in both directions).

---

### 9.2 Deferred-payment schedules without deferred-compensation-compliance language

**Pattern:** Severance is paid in installments spanning a significant period, payment timing depends on employee or employer discretion, or payment is linked to a "release effectiveness" date — and the agreement contains no language addressing compliance with deferred-compensation rules that some regimes impose on separation pay `[Verify current law]`.

**Why it matters:** Some tax regimes impose structural timing requirements on deferred separation payments, with consequences borne largely by the employee. Whether these rules apply, and whether the schedule complies, is strictly a question for tax counsel — the absence of any compliance language is simply a flag.

**What to check:**
- Record the payment schedule and every timing contingency verbatim — `[deadline verification required]` for any payment date logic; never compute one.
- Note whether any deferred-compensation-compliance or payment-timing savings clause is present.
- Route to `skills/tax/tax-issue-intake/SKILL.md` and flag for tax counsel `[Verify current law]`.

**Most exposed:** Employee (tax consequences of noncompliant timing); employer (correction and reporting exposure).

**Preferred position:** Payment characterization and schedule reviewed by a qualified tax professional before signing, with the agreement's allocation and timing language conformed to that advice — never drafted from this catalog.
**Fallback position:** If tax review cannot occur pre-signing, record the allocation and schedule as unverified, add the tax-review flag to the issues table as High priority, and route to `skills/tax/tax-issue-intake/SKILL.md`.

## 10. Process Patterns

### 10.1 Signature deadline pressure inconsistent with stated consideration periods

**Pattern:** A cover email, offer letter, or the agreement itself demands signature by a near-term date, while the agreement's own recitations describe a longer consideration period — or pressure is applied ("offer expires Friday") with no stated basis, or the stated deadline and the recited period simply do not line up on their face.

**Why it matters:** Pressure to sign faster than the agreement's own recited period undercuts the voluntariness the recitations are meant to establish — an employer-side validity exposure and an employee-side fairness flag. This catalog records the stated dates and periods; it never computes, reconciles, or validates them — `[deadline verification required]`, and any legal effect is for counsel `[Verify current law]`.

**What to check:**
- Record verbatim every stated deadline, consideration period, revocation period, and expiration date, with its source document.
- Flag any facial mismatch between a demanded signature date and a recited consideration period — as a mismatch, not a computed conclusion.
- Note any recitation that the employee "waives" the remaining consideration period by signing early, and flag it `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.
- Check whether changes to the agreement during negotiation purport to restart, or not restart, any stated period `[Verify current law]`.

**Most exposed:** Employee (pressured decision); employer (waiver-validity exposure).

---

### 10.2 Signing mechanics that undercut the recited process

**Pattern:** The agreement recites a considered, counseled process, but the mechanics contradict it: signature requested on the termination day itself, backdated effective dates, missing signature blocks for the employer, or an agreement presented only through a portal that logs acceptance without delivering a retainable copy.

**Why it matters:** Process defects are evidence in any later challenge to the release's validity and are cheap to fix before signing. For the employee, they signal a process worth slowing down.

**What to check:**
- Compare the recited process (time to consider, advice to consult counsel) against the actual presentation facts as the user describes them; label those facts as user-stated.
- Check for backdating, undated signature lines, or an effective date earlier than any revocation mechanics would allow — recorded as a facial inconsistency, `[deadline verification required]`.
- Confirm the employee can retain a complete copy, including all referenced attachments.

**Most exposed:** Employer (validity exposure); employee (process fairness).

**Preferred position:** Signing mechanics that match the recited process exactly — full stated consideration period honored, no expiration pressure inconsistent with it, counsel consultation encouraged in writing, and a complete retainable copy with all attachments.
**Fallback position:** If the employer will not extend a mismatched deadline, record the mismatch prominently in the issues table as High priority and escalate to counsel before signing — `[deadline verification required]`; deadline questions are always an attorney task.

## Reviewer Notes

- A red flag is a prompt to look closely and flag for attorney attention — not a conclusion that a clause is unenforceable, unlawful, or unacceptable.
- Whether a flagged pattern is a problem depends on the reviewing party, the jurisdiction, the claim mix, the termination context, and leverage — all of which are for the attorney to assess.
- Nothing in this catalog states what any jurisdiction requires or permits. Every jurisdiction-dependent point carries `[verify jurisdiction]` or `[Verify current law]`; every date or period is recorded, never computed — `[deadline verification required]`.
- The Preferred position / Fallback position lines are negotiation direction for counsel to weigh, not positions to assert to a user as advice.
- This catalog is not exhaustive. Unusual or matter-specific provisions not listed here may still warrant a flag in the issues table.
- See `skills/employment/severance-review/SKILL.md` for the full severance-review workflow.
- See `skills/contracts/references/red-flags.md` for general contract red-flag patterns (liability, disputes, governing law) that also appear in separation agreements.
- Tax-related patterns route to `skills/tax/tax-issue-intake/SKILL.md`.
