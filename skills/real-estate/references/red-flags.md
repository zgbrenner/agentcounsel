# Real-Estate Red-Flag Catalog

This catalog lists clause patterns and structural features that a reviewer should actively scan for when reviewing a real-estate document — a commercial lease, a purchase and sale agreement, or a financing package. Each entry describes the pattern, why it matters, what to check, and which client roles are most exposed. The catalog is organized by document family so that a review skill can run a Red Flags Quick Scan against the section family relevant to the document in front of it.

This catalog is a review aid. It identifies what to look for and why. Whether a flagged pattern is actually problematic depends on the specific document language, the client's role, the property, the transaction posture, the governing jurisdiction, and attorney judgment. Every flagged item is a candidate for attorney verification — not a legal conclusion. Nothing here states what the law requires, confirms title or lien priority, or computes a figure or a date.

Two disciplines are non-negotiable in this practice area and appear throughout the entries:

- **Never confirm title, lien priority, or marketability.** Whether a lien is perfected, first in priority, or free of prior encumbrances, and whether title is marketable, are matters for counsel and the title company. Flag every such point `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]` and route to `skills/real-estate/title-survey-objection-tracker`.
- **Never compute a figure or a deadline.** Rent, escalations, expense shares, prorations, prepayment premiums, interest, and reserve amounts are recorded as the document states them, never calculated. Every date is recorded as written and flagged `[deadline verification required]`. Property law is intensely local — flag `[verify jurisdiction]` wherever local law would govern.

## How to Use This Catalog

For each topic section relevant to the document under review:

1. Locate the relevant provision in the document (or note its absence — a missing provision is itself a red flag in many contexts).
2. Check the pattern description against the actual language, quoting operative terms verbatim rather than paraphrasing.
3. If the pattern is present, add the item to the risk matrix and the issue list, with a source citation.
4. Note which client roles are most exposed and whether the client's role matches the exposure profile.
5. Flag every finding for attorney review; do not resolve ambiguity, confirm title or priority, or compute any figure or date.

The sections are grouped by document family:

- **Commercial lease** — Sections 1–8.
- **Purchase and sale** — Sections 9–14.
- **Loan and financing** — Sections 15–18 (route deep loan analysis to `skills/real-estate/loan-document-review`).
- **Cross-cutting** — Sections 19–21 (title/lien priority, insurance, jurisdiction-specific property law).

Where a section carries a **Direction for counsel** line, it frames a preferred and a fallback direction the reviewing attorney may weigh — a direction of change, never drafted language, a recommendation, or a decision.

## 1. Lease — Rent, Escalation, and Operating-Expense / CAM Pass-Throughs

### 1.1 Uncapped or compounding escalation

**Pattern:** Base rent escalates by a fixed percentage that compounds annually, by an unbounded index reference, or by the greater of a fixed floor and an index — with no cap.

**Why it matters:** Over a long term, an uncapped or compounding escalator can move the effective rent far from where it started, and the tenant has no ceiling. The question is whether the escalation mechanism is bounded and how it interacts with renewal-term pricing.

**What to check:**
- Quote the escalation formula verbatim; do not compute the resulting rent.
- Is there a cap (annual and/or cumulative), a floor, or a "greater of" structure?
- Does the escalator carry into option/renewal terms, or reset?

**Most exposed:** Tenant on a long primary term; buyer or lender underwriting to a rent roll.

---

### 1.2 Operating-expense / CAM pass-through with weak controls

**Pattern:** The tenant pays its share of operating expenses or CAM on a base-year, expense-stop, or net basis, but the definition of includable costs is broad and the tenant's controls (caps, exclusions, gross-up, audit) are thin or absent.

**Why it matters:** A broad expense definition without exclusions (capital expenditures, capital reserves, management fees above market, landlord's financing costs, costs reimbursed by others) shifts landlord-side costs onto the tenant. A missing gross-up provision can distort the base year in a partially occupied building.

**What to check:**
- What is included in operating expenses / CAM, and what is expressly excluded?
- Is there a cap on controllable expenses, and how is "controllable" defined?
- Is there a gross-up provision, an audit right, and a defined tenant's-share/proportionate-share methodology?
- Is the base year or expense stop clearly defined?

**Most exposed:** Tenant paying pass-throughs; buyer relying on projected net income.

**Direction for counsel:** Preferred — a defined, exclusions-heavy expense pool with a controllable-expense cap, gross-up, and an audit right. Fallback — narrower exclusions with a cap and audit right preserved. Attorney sets the position against the client's leverage and the market `[verify jurisdiction]`.

---

### 1.3 Percentage rent and reporting

**Pattern:** The lease adds percentage rent on sales over a breakpoint, with broad definitions of "gross sales," limited exclusions, and audit/reporting obligations running against the tenant.

**Why it matters:** How "gross sales" is defined, what is excluded (returns, online sales, inter-store transfers, taxes), and how the breakpoint is set drive the tenant's exposure. The question is whether the definition and breakpoint are clearly bounded — not what the resulting rent will be.

**What to check:**
- Quote the "gross sales" definition and the breakpoint mechanics.
- What exclusions from gross sales are provided?
- What reporting, records-retention, and landlord audit rights apply to the tenant?

**Most exposed:** Retail tenant; landlord relying on percentage-rent upside.

## 2. Lease — Use, Exclusivity, and Co-Tenancy

### 2.1 Narrow permitted use with continuous-operation and go-dark constraints

**Pattern:** The permitted-use clause is narrow, and the lease pairs it with a continuous-operation ("keep open") covenant and restricts or prohibits the tenant going dark.

**Why it matters:** A narrow use clause limits the tenant's flexibility to change its business or assign; a continuous-operation covenant can force operation at a loss or trigger default/recapture. The interaction between use, continuous operation, and go-dark rights is the point to surface.

**What to check:**
- How narrowly is the permitted use defined, and is any use expressly prohibited?
- Is there a continuous-operation / keep-open covenant, and what is the remedy for breach?
- May the tenant go dark, and what triggers (recapture, termination, continued rent) follow?

**Most exposed:** Tenant needing operational flexibility; landlord protecting center vitality and co-tenancy.

---

### 2.2 Exclusive-use grant — scope, carve-outs, and remedies

**Pattern:** A tenant holds an exclusive-use right, or the lease grants exclusives to other tenants that bind this landlord/premises. Scope, carve-outs (existing tenants, incidental sales, anchors), and remedies vary widely.

**Why it matters:** An exclusive can constrain the landlord's leasing of the rest of the center and can be breached by the landlord unknowingly; a tenant relying on an exclusive needs enforceable remedies. The question is the exclusive's scope and enforceability, which is jurisdiction-sensitive.

**What to check:**
- Quote the exclusive's product/service scope and every carve-out.
- What are the remedies for violation (rent reduction, self-help, termination, damages)?
- Does the exclusive survive assignment, and does it bind successors? `[verify jurisdiction]`

**Most exposed:** Tenant relying on the exclusive; landlord whose leasing flexibility is constrained; buyer inheriting the obligation.

---

### 2.3 Co-tenancy conditions and triggers

**Pattern:** The tenant's obligations are conditioned on opening and/or ongoing co-tenancy (anchor presence, minimum occupancy), with remedies such as reduced/percentage-only rent or termination on a triggering event.

**Why it matters:** Co-tenancy provisions shift risk of center vacancy to the landlord and can materially reduce revenue; from the landlord/lender side they are an underwriting risk. The question is what triggers the remedy and how long a cure window runs.

**What to check:**
- What are the opening and ongoing co-tenancy thresholds, and how are they measured?
- What remedy follows a failure (alternate/percentage rent, then termination), and after what period? `[deadline verification required]`
- Is there a landlord cure/replacement-tenant right before the remedy vests?

**Most exposed:** Landlord and lender underwriting rent; tenant relying on the remedy.

## 3. Lease — Assignment and Subletting

### 3.1 Consent standard, recapture, and profit-sharing

**Pattern:** Assignment/subletting requires landlord consent; the lease may leave the consent standard silent, condition it on "sole discretion," and add recapture and profit-sharing on any transfer.

**Why it matters:** A silent or sole-discretion consent standard, broad recapture, and landlord capture of transfer profit constrain the tenant's exit and value; from the landlord side, a "not unreasonably withheld" standard plus permitted-transfer carve-outs erode control. The question is where the balance sits and how change-of-control is treated.

**What to check:**
- Is consent "not to be unreasonably withheld/conditioned/delayed," silent, or "sole discretion"?
- Does the landlord have recapture, and does it profit-share on subrent/assignment consideration?
- Are there permitted transfers (affiliates, successors, going-concern sale), and is change of control a deemed assignment?
- Does the assignor remain liable post-assignment?

**Most exposed:** Tenant needing transfer flexibility; landlord protecting tenant quality and upside; guarantor whose exposure may continue after transfer.

**Direction for counsel:** Preferred (tenant) — a reasonableness standard with defined criteria, permitted-transfer carve-outs, and no profit-sharing on affiliate transfers; (landlord) — consent control with recapture and profit-sharing preserved. Fallback — a reasonableness standard with limited recapture. Direction only, not drafted language.

## 4. Lease — Maintenance and Repair Allocation

### 4.1 Structural, roof, and systems obligations shifted to the tenant

**Pattern:** In a net or modified-net lease, obligations for the roof, structure, foundation, and building systems (HVAC, mechanical) are allocated to the tenant, or the allocation is ambiguous.

**Why it matters:** Structural and systems repair/replacement can be a significant, unbudgeted liability; in a single-tenant net lease the tenant may bear it entirely, while in a multi-tenant building an ambiguous allocation invites disputes. The question is which party bears capital-level repair and replacement, not what any repair will cost.

**What to check:**
- Who bears roof, structure, foundation, and systems repair — and replacement, as distinct from repair?
- Is there a distinction between repair and capital replacement, and any amortization of capital costs passed to the tenant?
- At a ground lease or triple-net, does the tenant bear compliance-driven capital work?
- What is the surrender/condition-on-return standard?

**Most exposed:** Net-lease tenant; buyer inheriting deferred maintenance; landlord if the allocation is ambiguous.

## 5. Lease — Casualty and Condemnation

### 5.1 Restoration, abatement, and termination rights

**Pattern:** Casualty and condemnation provisions allocate restoration duties, rent abatement, and termination rights unevenly — for example, a landlord termination right on major casualty with no matching tenant right, or abatement limited or excluded.

**Why it matters:** After a casualty or a taking, the parties need to know who must rebuild, whether rent abates during restoration, and who may walk. A one-sided termination right can leave a tenant without premises or a landlord without income. The question is the symmetry of the rights and the treatment of proceeds and awards.

**What to check:**
- Who is obligated to restore, and within what stated period? `[deadline verification required]`
- Does rent abate during restoration, and is abatement full or partial?
- Are termination rights (on major casualty, late-term casualty, or partial taking) mutual or one-sided?
- How are insurance proceeds and condemnation awards applied, and do lender requirements override? (see Sections 18, 20)

**Most exposed:** Tenant needing continuity and abatement; landlord/lender needing restoration and proceeds control.

## 6. Lease — Renewal, Expansion, and Termination Options

### 6.1 Option mechanics — trigger, window, and pricing

**Pattern:** Renewal, expansion, contraction, ROFO, or ROFR options turn on strict notice windows and pricing mechanisms (fixed, formula, or "fair market rent") that may be vague or one-sided, often with a condition that the tenant not be in default.

**Why it matters:** A missed notice window can forfeit a valuable option; a vague FMR mechanism invites dispute; a broad "no default" condition can let a minor default defeat the option. The question is whether the option is exercisable on clear terms — not when the window falls, which is never computed here.

**What to check:**
- Record the notice window and exercise mechanics exactly as written; flag `[deadline verification required]`. Do not compute the date.
- How is renewal/expansion pricing set (fixed, formula, FMR with an arbitration backstop)?
- What conditions gate exercise (no default, continuous occupancy, no assignment)?
- For ROFO/ROFR, what triggers the right and how long is the response window?

**Most exposed:** Tenant holding the option; landlord bound by it; buyer inheriting the encumbrance on future space.

## 7. Lease — SNDA, Estoppel, and Subordination Obligations

### 7.1 Automatic subordination without non-disturbance

**Pattern:** The lease automatically subordinates to current and future mortgages, and obligates the tenant to deliver estoppels and SNDAs on demand, but does not guarantee the tenant a non-disturbance agreement from the lender.

**Why it matters:** Automatic subordination without a non-disturbance commitment can leave a tenant subject to termination on foreclosure; broad estoppel obligations on short notice can force certifications the tenant has not verified. The question is whether non-disturbance is secured and whether estoppel/SNDA obligations are bounded. Deep review of the SNDA/estoppel itself routes to `skills/real-estate/estoppel-snda-review`.

**What to check:**
- Is subordination automatic/self-operative, or conditioned on delivery of an SNDA with non-disturbance?
- What must the tenant certify in an estoppel, on what notice, and how often? `[deadline verification required]`
- Does the lease cap the number of estoppels or the matters certified?
- For a lender/buyer, do the estoppel and SNDA obligations support the financing/acquisition structure?

**Most exposed:** Tenant facing subordination without non-disturbance; lender/buyer relying on estoppel and subordination.

## 8. Lease — Security Deposit and Personal Guaranty

### 8.1 Deposit application, burn-down, and return

**Pattern:** The security deposit (cash or letter of credit) is subject to broad landlord application rights, limited or no burn-down, and vague return conditions; an L/C may be drawable on broad triggers.

**Why it matters:** Broad application rights and vague return conditions can tie up tenant capital; a landlord relying on the deposit needs clear draw rights. The question is the scope of application, any burn-down, and the return mechanics — not the deposit amount, which is recorded as written.

**What to check:**
- Record the deposit amount and form as written; do not compute or opine on adequacy.
- What may the landlord apply the deposit to, and does it survive/replenish after application?
- Is there a burn-down tied to milestones, and what conditions govern return? `[deadline verification required]`
- For an L/C, what are the draw triggers and transfer/renewal obligations?

**Most exposed:** Tenant whose capital is committed; landlord relying on the deposit as security.

---

### 8.2 Personal or entity guaranty — scope, cap, and burn-down

**Pattern:** A guaranty backstops the lease; its scope (full-term vs. limited), any cap, burn-down, "good-guy" surrender conditions, and waivers of guarantor defenses vary widely and may be broader than the guarantor understood.

**Why it matters:** A full-term unconditional guaranty exposes the guarantor to the entire lease; a "good-guy" guaranty limits exposure if the tenant surrenders properly. The question is the guaranty's reach and how it can be reduced or released — never a conclusion on its enforceability.

**What to check:**
- Quote the guaranteed obligations, any cap, and any burn-down or "good-guy" surrender conditions.
- What guarantor defenses are waived, and does the guaranty survive assignment or lease amendment?
- What conditions (notice, surrender, keys) release or reduce the guaranty? `[verify jurisdiction]`

**Most exposed:** Guarantor; landlord relying on the guaranty as credit support.

**Direction for counsel:** Preferred (guarantor) — a capped or "good-guy" guaranty with clear release conditions and no waiver-driven expansion on amendment. Fallback — a burn-down tied to performance milestones. Enforceability is jurisdiction-specific `[verify jurisdiction]`; direction only.

## 9. Purchase & Sale — Deposit, Escrow, and Default Remedies

### 9.1 Deposit "going hard" and default-remedy asymmetry

**Pattern:** The earnest-money deposit becomes non-refundable ("hard") early or on staged milestones, and the default remedies are asymmetric — liquidated damages capped at the deposit for a buyer default, but specific performance and/or uncapped damages against the seller (or vice versa).

**Why it matters:** When the deposit goes hard and what each party's remedy is together determine who bears deal risk if the transaction fails. A buyer whose deposit is hard before diligence closes has little protection; a seller limited to the deposit may be under-compensated. The question is the timing and symmetry of remedies — not whether liquidated damages are enforceable, which is for counsel.

**What to check:**
- Record the deposit amount and each "hard" milestone as written; flag dates `[deadline verification required]`. Do not compute.
- Is the buyer's default remedy liquidated damages (deposit), and is it the sole/exclusive remedy?
- Is the seller's default remedy specific performance, damages, or capped return of deposit — and is it symmetric?
- Is the liquidated-damages clause a genuine pre-estimate or a penalty question flagged for counsel? `[verify jurisdiction]`

**Most exposed:** Buyer whose deposit hardens early; seller limited to a capped remedy.

**Direction for counsel:** Preferred (buyer) — deposit stays soft through diligence, liquidated damages capped at deposit both ways or specific performance available. Fallback — staged hardening with financing/title outs preserved. Direction only; enforceability is an attorney question `[verify jurisdiction]`.

---

### 9.2 Escrow holder, release conditions, and cost/risk allocation

**Pattern:** The escrow provisions leave the release conditions ambiguous, give one party unilateral instruction rights, or allocate escrow risk/cost unevenly.

**Why it matters:** Ambiguous release conditions can trap the deposit in an interpleader if the deal fails; unilateral instruction rights favor one side. The question is whether the release mechanics are clear and balanced.

**What to check:**
- Who is the escrow holder, and what are the exact conditions for release to each party?
- Can either party unilaterally instruct release, and what happens on a dispute?
- Who bears escrow costs and the risk of escrow-holder failure?

**Most exposed:** The party whose funds are at risk if release is contested.

---

## 10. Purchase & Sale — Due-Diligence / Inspection Period and Termination Rights

### 10.1 Diligence scope, access, and the free-look termination right

**Pattern:** The inspection period, access rights, and the buyer's right to terminate and recover the deposit are narrow, short, conditioned, or paired with a broad restoration/indemnity obligation for entry.

**Why it matters:** The diligence period and the unconditional right to terminate and recover the deposit are the buyer's core protection; if the window is short, access is limited, or termination is conditioned, the buyer's protection erodes. From the seller side, a long free-look with a refundable deposit ties up the property. The question is the scope of access and the cleanliness of the termination right — the expiry date is recorded, never computed.

**What to check:**
- Record the inspection-period length and expiry as written; flag `[deadline verification required]`. Do not compute.
- Is the termination right unconditional ("any reason or no reason") and does the deposit return in full?
- What are the access, insurance, restoration, and indemnity conditions for entry?
- Does third-party report reliance, estoppel receipt, or a financing/appraisal condition gate the free look?

**Most exposed:** Buyer relying on the free look; seller whose property is tied up.

## 11. Purchase & Sale — Title and Survey Objection Mechanics

### 11.1 Objection-and-cure mechanism and permitted exceptions

**Pattern:** The title/survey provisions set an objection window, a seller cure right (often permissive, not mandatory), a broad "permitted exceptions" basket, and a buyer's waive-or-terminate election — with terms that can leave the buyer taking title subject to unresolved matters.

**Why it matters:** How objections are raised, whether the seller must cure, what counts as a permitted exception, and the buyer's remedy if the seller will not cure together determine what encumbrances the buyer takes subject to. **This catalog never confirms title status, marketability, or lien priority** — those are for counsel and the title company. The question is whether the mechanism protects the reviewing party.

**What to check:**
- Record the objection window and cure/response window as written; flag `[deadline verification required]`.
- Is the seller's cure obligation mandatory, permissive, or capped at a stated cure amount?
- How broad is "permitted exceptions," and are monetary liens carved out for mandatory cure?
- What is the buyer's remedy if a title objection is not cured — waive, terminate, or offset?
- Route every title/priority/marketability question to `skills/real-estate/title-survey-objection-tracker` and flag `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]`.

**Most exposed:** Buyer taking title; lender relying on clear title for its lien.

**Direction for counsel:** Preferred (buyer) — mandatory cure of monetary liens and title matters the buyer objects to, with terminate-and-refund if uncured. Fallback — permissive cure with a full termination right. All title conclusions remain `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]` with the title company.

## 12. Purchase & Sale — Representations, Warranties, and Survival

### 12.1 Thin reps, broad knowledge qualifiers, and short survival

**Pattern:** The seller's representations are thin, heavily qualified by "to seller's knowledge" (with a narrow knowledge-party definition), and survive closing only briefly or not at all — sometimes with a floor/cap (basket) and a survival cutoff that limits post-closing recovery.

**Why it matters:** The breadth of the reps, the knowledge qualifiers, the survival period, and any basket/cap determine the buyer's post-closing recourse. Reps that do not survive, or survive briefly with a low cap, leave the buyer with little recovery for problems discovered after closing. The question is the scope and survival of recourse — not the enforceability of any limit.

**What to check:**
- What does the seller actually represent (title, leases, service contracts, litigation, environmental, compliance), and what is conspicuously absent?
- How is "seller's knowledge" defined, and to which individuals is it limited?
- What is the survival period, and is there a basket (floor) and cap on recovery? Record figures/dates as written. `[deadline verification required]`
- Does breach discovered pre-closing give a termination or closing-condition remedy?

**Most exposed:** Buyer relying on post-closing recourse; seller wanting a clean exit.

---

## 13. Purchase & Sale — Closing Conditions and Prorations

### 13.1 Closing conditions and financing/approval outs

**Pattern:** The conditions to each party's obligation to close (title, estoppels, tenant/lender consents, governmental approvals, no material adverse change, financing) are uneven, vague, or missing — for example, a seller with no meaningful conditions and a buyer whose financing is not a condition.

**Why it matters:** Closing conditions define who can walk and when. A missing financing condition exposes a buyer to a hard deposit with no loan; a vague estoppel or consent condition invites dispute at closing. The question is whether the conditions match the reviewing party's real contingencies.

**What to check:**
- List each party's conditions to close, and flag standard conditions that are absent.
- Is there a financing condition, and how does it interact with the deposit going hard? (see Section 9)
- Are required estoppels/SNDAs, tenant/lender consents, and approvals defined with a standard for sufficiency?
- What is the remedy if a condition fails — termination, extension, or waiver?

**Most exposed:** Buyer whose contingencies are not conditions; seller facing a soft-conditioned buyer.

---

### 13.2 Proration methodology and post-closing true-up

**Pattern:** The proration provisions (taxes, rents, CAM reconciliations, operating expenses, deposits, utilities) leave the proration date, methodology, or true-up mechanism ambiguous, or omit a category.

**Why it matters:** How and as of when items prorate, and whether there is a post-closing true-up, allocate real money between the parties. Ambiguity produces disputes after closing. The question is whether the methodology is complete and clear — the numbers are never computed here.

**What to check:**
- What is the proration date, and how is each category (taxes, rent, delinquent rent, CAM reconciliation, deposits) handled?
- Is there a post-closing true-up, and how long does it stay open? `[deadline verification required]`
- Are tenant security deposits credited to the buyer, and how are prepaid rents treated?

**Most exposed:** Either party; the side disadvantaged by an ambiguous or omitted category.

---

## 14. Purchase & Sale — "As-Is" Disclaimers and Casualty Before Closing

### 14.1 "As-is / where-is" disclaimer and release

**Pattern:** The buyer takes the property "as-is, where-is," with a broad disclaimer of all warranties and a release/waiver of claims (sometimes including environmental and latent-defect claims), leaving few or no seller reps to rely on.

**Why it matters:** A broad "as-is" clause plus a release can foreclose the buyer's recourse for defects discovered after closing, and interacts directly with the thinness of the reps (Section 12). The question is what recourse survives the disclaimer, not whether the disclaimer is enforceable.

**What to check:**
- Quote the "as-is" and release language, and identify exactly which claims are released or waived.
- Do any reps, indemnities, or fraud/intentional-misrepresentation carve-outs survive the disclaimer?
- Does the release reach environmental and latent-defect claims, and is that consistent with the diligence the buyer could do?

**Most exposed:** Buyer relying on the property's condition; seller seeking a clean exit.

---

### 14.2 Casualty and condemnation before closing

**Pattern:** The pre-closing casualty/condemnation provision sets a materiality threshold for the buyer's termination right, and allocates insurance proceeds, condemnation awards, and deductibles — sometimes forcing the buyer to close with an assignment of proceeds regardless of the loss.

**Why it matters:** A loss between signing and closing raises who bears it and whether the buyer must still close. A low or undefined materiality threshold can force a buyer to close on damaged property; a high one lets a buyer walk on minor damage. The question is the threshold and the proceeds allocation.

**What to check:**
- What is the materiality threshold for a buyer termination right, and how is it measured? Record as written; do not compute.
- Are insurance proceeds and condemnation awards assigned to the buyer, with a credit for deductibles/uninsured loss?
- Does a lender requirement (Section 18) override the proceeds allocation?

**Most exposed:** Buyer required to close on a damaged asset; seller wanting a firm deal.

## 15. Loan / Financing — Recourse Carve-Outs ("Bad-Boy" Triggers)

> Deep analysis of a financing package — the loan agreement, note, mortgage/deed of trust, assignment of leases and rents, guaranty, and environmental indemnity — belongs in `skills/real-estate/loan-document-review`. The entries in Sections 15–18 are a scan aid, not a substitute for that review. Never confirm enforceability of a carve-out or a remedy — quote it and flag `[verify jurisdiction]`.

### 15.1 Springing full recourse vs. loss-only carve-outs

**Pattern:** A non-recourse loan carries recourse carve-outs ("bad-boy" provisions). Some trigger loss-only recourse (liability for the loss caused); others "spring" the entire debt into full recourse. Broad or springing triggers — a prohibited transfer, subordinate debt, an SPE-covenant breach, or even a contested bankruptcy — can convert a non-recourse loan into full recourse.

**Why it matters:** The line between loss-only and springing full recourse, and the breadth of the triggers, determine the guarantor's true exposure. A broad "solvency" or "no additional debt" trigger can spring full recourse on ordinary business events. The question is the scope of the triggers and which produce full vs. loss-only recourse — never a conclusion on enforceability.

**What to check:**
- Quote each carve-out verbatim, and classify it as loss-only or springing full recourse.
- Do any triggers spring full recourse on events partly outside the borrower's control (involuntary bankruptcy, third-party lien)?
- Is the guaranty's reach consistent with the carve-outs it is meant to backstop?
- Flag broad/springing triggers prominently and route deeper analysis to `skills/real-estate/loan-document-review`. `[verify jurisdiction]`

**Most exposed:** Guarantor/borrower principal exposed to springing recourse; lender relying on the carve-outs.

**Direction for counsel:** Preferred (borrower/guarantor) — loss-only recourse for most carve-outs, springing full recourse limited to voluntary bankruptcy and true fraud. Fallback — narrowed springing triggers with cure rights. Enforceability is jurisdiction-specific `[verify jurisdiction]`; direction only.

## 16. Loan / Financing — Covenants and Transfer / Due-on-Sale Restrictions

### 16.1 Transfer, due-on-sale, and change-of-control restrictions

**Pattern:** The loan restricts transfers of the property and of direct/indirect ownership interests, secondary/mezzanine financing, and change of control, with a due-on-sale/due-on-encumbrance acceleration right — and any permitted-transfer or assumption provision is narrow or heavily conditioned.

**Why it matters:** Broad transfer and due-on-sale restrictions can block ordinary estate planning, entity restructuring, or a sale, and can accelerate the loan on an inadvertent transfer. The question is the reach of the restrictions and the availability of permitted transfers and assumption — not whether acceleration is enforceable.

**What to check:**
- Quote the transfer definition, including indirect/upstream transfers and change-of-control thresholds.
- Are there permitted transfers (estate planning, affiliates, minority interests), and what conditions gate them?
- Is loan assumption permitted, and on what conditions and fees? Record fees as written; do not compute.
- Is secondary/mezzanine financing prohibited or conditioned, and does it trigger recourse (Section 15)?

**Most exposed:** Borrower needing transfer/restructuring flexibility; guarantor exposed via a transfer carve-out.

---

### 16.2 Financial, SPE, and reporting covenants

**Pattern:** The loan imposes financial covenants (DSCR, debt-yield, LTV, net-worth/liquidity), single-purpose-entity and separateness covenants, and reporting covenants — some tested on stated formulas, some ongoing.

**Why it matters:** A covenant breach can trigger a cash sweep, a reserve, or an event of default. SPE/separateness breaches can also spring recourse (Section 15). The question is what the covenants require and what a breach triggers — the ratios are recorded as written and never tested or computed here.

**What to check:**
- Record each financial covenant's formula and test date as written; flag `[deadline verification required]`. Do not compute or test.
- What does a covenant breach trigger — cash sweep, reserve, default, or recourse?
- Are SPE/separateness covenants present, and are they tied to the recourse carve-outs?
- What are the reporting obligations and their deadlines? `[deadline verification required]`

**Most exposed:** Borrower operating close to a covenant threshold; guarantor exposed through an SPE-linked carve-out.

## 17. Loan / Financing — Reserves, Cash Management, and Prepayment

### 17.1 Cash-management, lockbox, and reserve triggers

**Pattern:** The loan requires reserves/escrows (tax, insurance, replacement, TI/LC, debt-service) and a lockbox/cash-management structure that can spring from a soft or hard lockbox to a full cash sweep on defined triggers (DSCR failure, tenant rollover, default).

**Why it matters:** A springing cash sweep can trap the borrower's cash flow on a covenant slip, and large upfront/ongoing reserves affect liquidity. The question is what triggers the sweep and how trapped cash is released — reserve amounts are recorded as written, never computed.

**What to check:**
- Record each reserve's funding amount and schedule as written; flag `[deadline verification required]`. Do not compute.
- What triggers a cash sweep, and what conditions release trapped cash back to the borrower?
- Is the lockbox soft, hard, or springing, and who controls the account?

**Most exposed:** Borrower whose liquidity depends on cash flow; lender relying on the reserve/sweep structure.

---

### 17.2 Prepayment lockout, yield maintenance, defeasance, and exit fees

**Pattern:** The loan restricts prepayment through a lockout period, a yield-maintenance or prepayment premium, a defeasance requirement, and/or an exit fee, limiting the borrower's ability to refinance or sell.

**Why it matters:** Prepayment restrictions can make an early refinance or sale expensive or impossible during the lockout, affecting the borrower's flexibility and the deal's economics. The question is the structure and duration of the restriction — the premium is recorded as a formula and never computed.

**What to check:**
- Quote the lockout period, the prepayment-premium/yield-maintenance formula, and any defeasance mechanics as written. Do not compute the premium.
- Is there an open prepayment window before maturity, and are partial prepayments permitted?
- Is there an exit fee, and does it stack with the prepayment premium?
- How does a casualty/condemnation prepayment interact with the premium?

**Most exposed:** Borrower planning an early sale/refinance; guarantor if prepayment default springs recourse.

**Direction for counsel:** Preferred (borrower) — a shorter lockout, an open window before maturity, and a casualty/condemnation prepayment carve-out from the premium. Fallback — yield maintenance with an open period and defeasance flexibility. Direction only; the premium is never computed here.

## 18. Loan / Financing — Cross-Default and Remedies

### 18.1 Cross-default, cross-collateralization, and remedy stacking

**Pattern:** An event of default under one document is a default under all; the loan is cross-collateralized with other properties/loans; and the remedies (acceleration, foreclosure, receiver, ALR enforcement, guaranty pursuit) stack, sometimes without notice or cure for defined defaults.

**Why it matters:** Cross-default and cross-collateralization can let a problem at one property trigger default and remedies across a portfolio; thin notice/cure rights leave little room to fix a slip. The question is the reach of cross-default and the availability of notice and cure — never a conclusion on remedy enforceability.

**What to check:**
- Which documents and which other loans/properties are cross-defaulted or cross-collateralized?
- Which events of default carry notice and cure, and which are immediate? Record cure periods as written; flag `[deadline verification required]`.
- What remedies are available (acceleration, foreclosure/power of sale, receiver, ALR enforcement), and how do they interact with the guaranties?
- Flag remedy availability `[verify jurisdiction]` and route deeper analysis to `skills/real-estate/loan-document-review`.

**Most exposed:** Borrower/guarantor with a multi-property relationship; lender relying on stacked remedies.

## 19. Cross-Cutting — Title and Lien-Priority Questions

### 19.1 Any title, priority, or marketability question

**Pattern:** Any document — a lease's subordination clause, a PSA's title provisions, a mortgage's lien grant — raises a question about whether title is good/marketable, whether a lien is perfected, or where it sits in priority.

**Why it matters:** Title and lien-priority conclusions drive whether a buyer gets what it is paying for and whether a lender's security is worth what it expects. **This catalog, and every skill that uses it, never confirms title status, marketability, lien perfection, or priority.** Those are matters for counsel and the title company. The question is only to identify and route the issue.

**What to check:**
- Identify every point where the document assumes or asserts good title, perfection, or a priority position.
- Flag each such point `[ATTORNEY TO CONFIRM]` and route to `skills/real-estate/title-survey-objection-tracker`.
- Note any reliance on a title commitment, policy, or endorsement that was not provided.
- Never state that title is good, a lien is perfected, or priority is established.

**Most exposed:** Buyer relying on marketable title; lender relying on lien priority.

## 20. Cross-Cutting — Insurance Requirements

### 20.1 Insurance obligations, evidence, and lender/landlord protections

**Pattern:** A lease or loan imposes insurance obligations (coverage types, limits, additional-insured/mortgagee status, waivers of subrogation, evidence and notice) that may be incomplete, mismatched to the risks, or missing operational mechanics.

**Why it matters:** Insurance backstops indemnities and casualty risk; a bare "carry insurance" requirement without additional-insured/mortgagee status, certificates, and cancellation notice gives the protected party no real rights against the policy. Detailed analysis of insurance adequacy is out of scope for the review skills.

**What to check:**
- Identify the required coverages, limits, and additional-insured/mortgagee/loss-payee designations, recorded as written.
- Are certificates, waivers of subrogation, and cancellation/material-change notice required?
- Do the required coverages match the risks the property and the deal create?
- Route detailed insurance analysis to `skills/insurance/insurance-requirements-contract-review`.

**Most exposed:** Party relying on the counterparty's insurance as a real source of recovery (landlord, lender, buyer).

## 21. Cross-Cutting — Jurisdiction-Specific Property-Law Flags

### 21.1 Local property, recording, and remedy law

**Pattern:** The document turns on rules that are intensely local — recording and priority, transfer and mortgage tax, foreclosure/power-of-sale procedure, mechanics'-lien exposure, redemption rights, usury, deed form, homestead, community property, and landlord-tenant remedies.

**Why it matters:** Property law varies sharply by jurisdiction, and a provision that is standard in one state may be void, differently construed, or subject to a mandatory procedure in another. The catalog never supplies the local rule — it flags where local law governs so counsel can verify it.

**What to check:**
- Identify each provision whose effect depends on local property, recording, tax, or remedy law, and flag `[verify jurisdiction]`.
- Note where the governing-law clause and the property's situs differ, since property remedies typically follow the situs.
- Flag transfer/mortgage-tax, recording, and foreclosure-procedure questions for counsel; never supply the rule, the rate, or the deadline.
- Record every date `[deadline verification required]`; never compute a limitations, redemption, or recording period.

**Most exposed:** Any party relying on a provision whose enforceability or procedure is set by local law.
