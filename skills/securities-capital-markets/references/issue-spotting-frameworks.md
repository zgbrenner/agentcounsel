# Securities Issue-Spotting Frameworks

This catalog organizes the practitioner-known issue-spotting frameworks the securities-capital-markets skills draw on. Each section walks the structure of a real securities-law analysis — decision points, candidate paths, facts that must be in the record before a path is even a candidate — without applying the framework to any specific facts and without concluding that any path, exemption, or treatment is available.

Securities-law rule architecture has been amended multiple times in recent years. The Securities Act, Exchange Act, and the SEC's rules under Regulations A, CF, D, and S — together with rules under §16, §13(d)/(g), Rule 10b5-1, Rule 144, and the integration framework — have all seen substantive amendments at various points. This reference therefore carries `[verify current SEC rule version at time of review]` markers liberally. **Do not rely on the framework descriptions below without confirming the current rule text with counsel.** Every framework reference in this catalog is a candidate for attorney verification — never a legal conclusion.

State blue-sky law overlays federal securities law at every point that follows. Federal preemption applies only where the federal statute or rule explicitly preempts (most notably, NSMIA preemption for "covered securities," including Rule 506(b)/(c) offerings — preempts substantive blue-sky regulation but does not preempt notice filings and fees). Every section below assumes a state-by-state overlay until counsel confirms otherwise.

This catalog is a review aid. It identifies the decision points and what to look for; it does not advise on outcome.

## How to Use This Catalog

For any securities skill:

1. Identify which framework section(s) below are in scope for the deliverable being produced.
2. Walk the decision points in order. At each, record the user-supplied facts and any open facts.
3. Where a framework references a specific SEC rule, carry the `[verify current SEC rule version at time of review]` marker — do not pin the analysis to a specific amendment date.

## A. Private Offering Exemption Decision Tree

A framework for surfacing candidate exemption pathways for a contemplated or in-progress private offering. The skill walks the tree; the attorney chooses the path.

### A.1 First-cut gate: is the offering an "offer" or "sale" of a "security" in a jurisdiction that requires registration absent an exemption?

**Facts to record:**
- Instrument description (equity, convertible, debt, SAFE, token, profit interest, LLC interest, partnership interest, joint-venture interest, other).
- Whether the instrument analysis itself (the "is it a security" question) is contested or unsettled — particularly for tokens, profit interests, and joint-venture interests `[verify current SEC framework]`.
- The jurisdictions where offers will be made and where each anticipated purchaser will be located.

The "is it a security" determination is for the attorney. The skill records the facts that support the determination, never the determination itself.

### A.2 General solicitation and general advertising

**Decision point:** Are general solicitation and general advertising contemplated, threatened, or already occurring?

This is the most important gate in the private-offering tree.

**Facts to record:**
- Channels used or contemplated (mass email, public website, social media, press release, conferences open to the public, demo days, paid advertising, "rolling" deck distribution to investors with whom the issuer lacks a pre-existing substantive relationship).
- Whether each prospective investor has a "pre-existing substantive relationship" with the issuer or its placement agent — and whether the relationship pre-dates the offering. The substantive-relationship analysis is highly fact-dependent and contested at the margins `[verify current SEC interpretive guidance]`.
- Whether the issuer is contemplating a "demo day" or accelerator-pitch event and the structure of that event.
- Whether the issuer or placement agent has issued any "testing-the-waters" communications and under which framework.

**Why this matters:** General solicitation eliminates Rule 506(b) as a candidate path. Where general solicitation is present, the candidate paths shift to Rule 506(c), Reg A+, Reg CF, or an offering structured around a category that permits or contemplates solicitation. If general solicitation has already occurred and the issuer wishes to claim 506(b), that is a critical attorney issue.

### A.3 Rule 506(b) candidate path

**Candidate-path tests:**
- No general solicitation or general advertising in the offering.
- Sales limited to "accredited investors" plus up to 35 "sophisticated" non-accredited investors who receive specified disclosure.
- All investors meet the conditions in Regulation D Rule 501 et seq. `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

**Facts to record:**
- Verification posture for accredited status (reliance on investor questionnaires and representations; documentation retained).
- Number and identity of any non-accredited purchasers; the disclosure delivered to them.
- Whether a private placement memorandum or analogous disclosure has been delivered.
- Bad-actor diligence universe (see A.7).
- Integration analysis facts (see A.8).
- Form D filing posture (see Section E).

**Why this matters:** 506(b) is a federal-preemptive safe harbor under Reg D `[verify current SEC rule version]` but is conditional. Loss of the conditions takes the issuer outside 506(b); the offering may still be exempt under §4(a)(2) but loses the bright-line safe harbor.

### A.4 Rule 506(c) candidate path

**Candidate-path tests:**
- General solicitation and general advertising are permitted.
- All purchasers are accredited investors.
- The issuer takes "reasonable steps to verify" accredited-investor status — a higher burden than 506(b) and not satisfied by investor self-certification alone `[verify current SEC rule version, including any safe-harbor verification methods added by amendment]`.

**Facts to record:**
- Verification method contemplated (income/asset documentation review; CPA/attorney/registered broker-dealer/SEC-registered investment-adviser letter; third-party verification service; reliance on a permitted safe-harbor verification method `[verify current SEC rule version]`).
- Whether sufficient documentation will be (or has been) collected and retained.
- Solicitation channel inventory (see A.2).
- Bad-actor diligence universe (see A.7).
- Integration analysis facts (see A.8).
- Form D filing posture (see Section E).

**Why this matters:** 506(c) permits broader marketing but raises the verification burden. The choice between 506(b) and 506(c) is an attorney call and is often driven by the issuer's marketing posture, not by a post-hoc choice of safe harbor. Misalignment between actual marketing conduct and the claimed safe harbor is a recurring enforcement issue.

### A.5 Reg S candidate path

**Candidate-path tests:**
- Offers and sales are made in an "offshore transaction" with no "directed selling efforts" in the United States.
- Reg S has a categorical structure (Category 1, Category 2, Category 3) keyed to the issuer's nature and the security type, with progressively more restrictive distribution-compliance-period requirements `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

**Facts to record:**
- Each anticipated purchaser's location and the offering channel used to reach each.
- Whether the issuer is a "domestic" or "foreign" issuer and whether the security is "fungible" with a U.S. trading market — both of which affect category classification.
- Distribution-compliance-period posture (length, restrictions on resale into the U.S., legending, transfer-agent stop instructions) `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Whether Reg S is being claimed alongside a Reg D offering (concurrent / "side-by-side" structure) and the integration considerations that follow (see A.8).

**Why this matters:** Reg S applies to offshore offers and sales; it does not exempt onshore offers. The categorical classification drives the distribution-compliance-period mechanics. Reg S/Reg D side-by-side offerings are common but raise integration questions the attorney must work through.

### A.6 Other candidate paths

The decision tree includes additional paths the skill should surface where the facts indicate candidacy. Each path's availability is an attorney question.

**Reg A+ (Tier 1 / Tier 2):**
- An exemption with significant ongoing-disclosure and qualification requirements; Tier 1 and Tier 2 differ in offering ceiling, state-preemption posture, and reporting burden `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Facts to record: offering size, contemplated investor mix, ongoing-reporting capacity of the issuer, testing-the-waters posture.

**Reg CF (crowdfunding):**
- Conducted through a registered funding portal or broker-dealer; ceiling and individual-investor-limit mechanics; required disclosures and ongoing-reporting obligations `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Facts to record: portal or broker-dealer counterparty, offering size, contemplated investor profile, marketing posture.

**Intrastate (Rule 147 / Rule 147A):**
- Offers and sales limited to residents of a single state; the issuer must meet doing-business-in-state tests; resale restrictions follow `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Facts to record: each investor's state of residence, the issuer's doing-business posture in the state, marketing channels.

**Section 4(a)(2) (without 506 safe harbor):**
- The statutory private-placement exemption underlying Reg D; reliance without the 506 safe harbor sacrifices the safe-harbor bright line and the state-preemption benefit, but may be the only candidate path where a 506 condition fails.

**Rule 144 and Rule 144A:** Resale frameworks, not primary-offering exemptions, but the skill should record the facts that affect resale posture (legend obligations, holding-period mechanics, QIB analysis).

### A.7 Bad-actor disqualification under Rule 506(d)

Rule 506(d) disqualifies a Reg D 506 offering where any "covered person" has a "disqualifying event" within specified look-back periods `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

**Facts to record for the attorney:**
- The universe of "covered persons" — issuer, predecessors, affiliates, directors, executive officers and other officers participating in the offering, 20% beneficial owners, general partners and managing members, investment managers, promoters connected with the issuer in any capacity, persons compensated for solicitation, and the directors and executive officers of each. The skill builds the list; the attorney confirms inclusion.
- Each covered person's adverse-event history within the relevant look-back periods (criminal convictions, court injunctions and restraining orders, SEC and other regulatory orders, suspensions and expulsions from self-regulatory organizations, postal-service false-representation orders) `[verify current SEC rule version for look-back periods]`.
- Disclosure-vs-disqualification distinction: events occurring before September 23, 2013 generally trigger disclosure rather than disqualification `[verify current SEC interpretive guidance]`; the precise treatment is for counsel.
- Reasonable-care defense facts (the issuer's diligence process, written questionnaires, certifications).

**Why this matters:** Rule 506(d) disqualification is offering-fatal; the attorney must verify the universe and conduct the look-back. The skill never concludes disqualification or non-disqualification.

### A.8 Integration analysis

Integration tests whether two or more nominally separate offerings should be treated as a single offering for exemption purposes. The integration framework has been amended substantively `[verify current SEC integration rule version]`; the skill records the facts and routes the analysis.

**Facts to record:**
- Inventory of prior offerings within the rolling look-back the current integration framework applies `[verify current SEC integration rule version]`.
- For each prior offering: exemption claimed, security type, purchasers, dollar size, completion date, marketing channels, common-plan-of-financing indicia.
- Inventory of contemplated future offerings and the same data points.
- Common-plan-of-financing facts: same security or different? same class of purchasers? same general purpose for proceeds? same channels?
- Safe-harbor facts under the current integration framework (e.g., post-offering safe harbors based on elapsed time, terminated-offering safe harbors) `[verify current SEC integration rule version]`.

**Why this matters:** Integration can collapse a 506(b) offering into a prior generally-solicited offering and destroy the 506(b) condition; it can also affect Reg S/Reg D side-by-side structures and intrastate offerings. The integration analysis is an attorney task.

### A.10 Section 4(a)(2) (without Rule 506 safe harbor)

Where a Rule 506 condition fails or is not relied upon, the underlying statutory §4(a)(2) exemption remains. The standard is broadly that the issuer makes offers and sales not involving any public offering.

**Facts to record:**
- The number and identity of all offerees (not just purchasers) — §4(a)(2) analysis turns on offers, not just sales.
- Each offeree's sophistication, financial wherewithal, and access to information about the issuer.
- The information actually furnished to each offeree.
- The manner of offering (private channels, pre-existing relationships, no general advertising).
- Resale restrictions and legending.

**Why this matters:** §4(a)(2) without 506 sacrifices the bright-line conditions and the NSMIA preemption that follows from a 506 offering's NSMIA-covered-securities status. State blue-sky law applies on a substantive basis. The skill never asserts §4(a)(2) is available; it surfaces the facts.

### A.11 Rule 144 (affiliate and non-affiliate resales)

Rule 144 is a resale framework, not a primary-offering exemption, but it interacts with primary-offering posture in many situations (legending, restricted-stock holding, registration-statement availability).

**Facts to record:**
- Whether the holder is an "affiliate" of the issuer or has been an affiliate within the relevant period `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- The holding-period facts (date of acquisition, manner of acquisition, prior holders in chain).
- Whether the issuer is a "reporting company" and current in its periodic filings.
- Whether the sale is contemplated through a broker, by the holder, or otherwise.
- Volume limitations applicable to affiliates `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Form 144 filing posture (where required).

### A.12 Rule 144A (QIB resales)

Rule 144A is a resale safe harbor for sales to qualified institutional buyers (QIBs). It does not exempt offerings to non-QIBs.

**Facts to record:**
- Each prospective purchaser's QIB status and the basis for the QIB determination `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- The information the issuer is required to make available under Rule 144A's information condition (where applicable).
- The interaction with concurrent Reg S or Reg D offerings.
- Resale and notice-of-resale-restriction posture.

### A.13 State blue-sky overlay

Every candidate federal path carries a state-by-state overlay analysis. The skill builds the state-of-sale map; the attorney runs the state-by-state framework.

**Facts to record per state of sale:**
- Whether NSMIA preempts substantive regulation in the candidate path (Rule 506(b)/(c) offerings are NSMIA-covered securities for substantive regulation; Reg A+ Tier 2 is preempted for substantive regulation; Tier 1, Reg CF, and intrastate offerings are not) `[verify current SEC rule version and NSMIA preemption scope]`.
- Whether the state requires a notice filing notwithstanding preemption (most states do for 506 offerings).
- Fee and consent-to-service-of-process posture.
- Timing of the state notice filing relative to first sale `[deadline verification required]` — surfaced as a fact, never computed.

See Section E for the full Form D and blue-sky tracking framework.

### A.14 Investment-company status overlay

Where the issuer holds investment securities of a magnitude that may trigger Investment Company Act analysis, the §3(a)(1) investment-company definition and the §3(b)/§3(c) exclusions must be considered.

**Facts to record:**
- The issuer's asset composition (investment securities vs. operating assets).
- The user's view of which §3(c) exclusion (if any) is the candidate (e.g., §3(c)(1), §3(c)(7)) and the facts supporting that candidate.
- The number and category of beneficial owners and whether the candidate exclusion's investor limits are at risk of being exceeded.

The skill never concludes investment-company status; it surfaces the facts.

## B. Offering-Document Disclosure Framework

A framework for reviewing the disclosure in an S-1, S-3, S-4, prospectus supplement, private placement memorandum (PPM), offering memorandum (OM), or other offering document. The skill records gaps and patterns; the attorney concludes adequacy.

### B.1 Risk-factor specificity

**Pattern to flag — boilerplate without issuer-specific content:** A risk factor that recites a generic industry risk without naming the issuer's specific exposure (named customers, dollar magnitudes, geographies, regulatory regimes by jurisdiction, contractual counterparties).

**Why it matters:** Boilerplate risk factors are themselves a flag under the SEC's risk-factor architecture, which requires that risk factors be specific to the registrant `[verify current SEC rule version]`. They also create downstream consistency issues where boilerplate language fails to track changes in the business.

**What to check:**
- Each risk factor for whether it names the issuer's actual circumstances.
- Whether quantitative magnitudes (customer concentration percentages, contract dollar values, geographic revenue mix) appear where they would be meaningful.
- Whether new business developments have triggered an update to existing risk factors.
- Whether a risk factor is duplicative of another in the document.

### B.2 Material developments not yet disclosed

**Pattern to flag:** Facts the user has surfaced (litigation, regulatory inquiry, material customer loss, supply-chain disruption, cybersecurity incident, management change, restatement consideration, going-concern indicator) that have not been integrated into the risk factors, MD&A, or other affected sections.

**What to check:**
- Each user-surfaced material development against each section of the offering document where it would be expected to appear.
- Whether prior periodic filings disclosed the development and whether the offering document is consistent.
- Whether the development triggers a separate ongoing-disclosure obligation (e.g., 8-K) — record as a fact for attorney routing.

### B.3 MD&A consistency with financial statements

**What to check:**
- Each numerical assertion in the MD&A against the corresponding line in the financial statements.
- Each known-trend assertion in the MD&A against the supporting facts.
- Whether the MD&A discusses each segment, geography, or product line that the financial statements disclose separately.
- Whether non-GAAP measures used in the MD&A are reconciled per the SEC's non-GAAP framework `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

### B.4 Forward-looking statements and the PSLRA safe-harbor framework

**What to check:**
- Whether the offering document identifies forward-looking statements and accompanies them with meaningful cautionary statements.
- Whether the PSLRA safe harbor is available to this issuer / this offering / this category of statements (the safe harbor has carve-outs — IPO issuers, certain offering documents, certain bad-actor situations `[verify current statutory and SEC rule version]`).
- Whether forward-looking statements in the offering document are consistent with prior periodic filings.

### B.5 Use of proceeds specificity

**What to check:**
- Whether the use-of-proceeds section identifies specific uses or relies on "general corporate purposes" language.
- Whether specific dollar amounts are allocated.
- Whether contemplated acquisitions or material transactions are disclosed at the appropriate level of specificity.

### B.6 Related-party transactions

**What to check:**
- Whether all transactions meeting the SEC's related-party threshold are disclosed `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Whether the disclosure includes the parties, the nature of the relationship, the dollar amounts, and material terms.
- Whether transactions identified in board minutes or governance materials but not in the document are unintentionally omitted.

### B.7 Going-concern and capitalization

**What to check:**
- Whether the auditors' report includes a going-concern qualification or whether facts in the document support one even where the audit report does not.
- Whether the capitalization table is current as of the offering date and reconciles to the financial statements.
- Whether dilution disclosure is present for offerings where SEC rules require it.

### B.8 Cybersecurity, AI, privacy, and data-protection disclosure

The SEC has expanded disclosure expectations for cybersecurity risk management, incident disclosure, AI use and risk, privacy and data-protection programs, and related governance `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

**What to check:**
- Whether cybersecurity risk-management and governance disclosure tracks the current required architecture.
- Whether material cybersecurity incidents within the reporting period are disclosed at the current required level of specificity.
- Whether AI-related risks (development, deployment, third-party reliance, regulatory exposure) are identified to the extent material.
- Whether privacy and data-protection programs are described in terms consistent with regulatory filings, certifications, and prior public statements.

### B.9 Critical accounting estimates and policies

**What to check:**
- Whether the document identifies each estimate that is both material and inherently uncertain.
- Whether the disclosure walks the assumptions, the alternative outcomes, and the sensitivity.
- Whether the discussion is consistent with the notes to the financial statements.

### B.10 Liquidity and capital resources

**What to check:**
- Whether the discussion identifies primary sources of liquidity and known material commitments.
- Whether debt-covenant compliance, including projected compliance over the relevant horizon, is addressed.
- Whether material capital commitments and contingent obligations are quantified.
- Whether known trends or uncertainties bearing on liquidity are discussed.

### B.11 Selling-stockholders disclosure (where applicable)

**What to check:**
- For resale offerings, whether each selling stockholder's beneficial ownership before and after the offering is disclosed.
- Whether any selling stockholder is an affiliate of the issuer and the disclosure tracks that status.
- Whether the resale registration form (e.g., S-3) is available to the selling stockholder.

## C. SEC Filing Consistency Framework

A framework for cross-checking a set of SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, S-1/S-3/S-4, proxy, prospectus supplement, Form D) for internal and cross-filing consistency. The skill records inconsistencies; the attorney concludes materiality.

### C.1 Cross-filing item map

For each pair of filings in scope, record the items that appear in both and should reconcile:

| Item | 10-K | 10-Q | 8-K | S-1/3/4 | Proxy | Form D |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Business description | ✓ | (changes) | (M&A) | ✓ | (background) | (issuer description) |
| Risk factors | ✓ | (updates) | — | ✓ | — | — |
| MD&A narrative | ✓ | ✓ | — | ✓ | — | — |
| Financial-statement figures | ✓ | ✓ | (selected) | ✓ | (selected) | — |
| Share counts | ✓ | ✓ | (changes) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Executive/director information | ✓ | (changes) | (changes) | ✓ | ✓ | (executive officers) |
| Related-party disclosures | ✓ | (interim) | — | ✓ | ✓ | — |
| Material contracts | ✓ | (new) | (new) | ✓ | — | — |
| Forward-looking statements | ✓ | ✓ | (selected) | ✓ | (selected) | — |

This is a starting matrix; the actual matrix depends on the filings in scope and the registrant's circumstances `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

### C.2 Numerical consistency checks

**What to check:**
- Each financial-statement figure cited outside the financial statements (in MD&A, business description, risk factors, selected-data tables, exhibits, earnings releases attached as 8-K exhibits, proxy summaries) against the underlying financial statements.
- Each percentage or ratio computed from financial-statement figures.
- Each share count cited outside the capitalization table.
- Each backward-looking comparison (year-over-year, quarter-over-quarter) for consistency with prior filings.

### C.3 Narrative consistency checks

**What to check:**
- Business-description narrative across filings for consistency in segment/geography/product-line treatment.
- Risk-factor language for additions, deletions, or changes that should be flagged for attorney review.
- MD&A trend discussions across periods.
- Forward-looking statements for inconsistency (a statement in one filing about future capex levels that does not match another filing's discussion).
- Defined-term consistency across the filing set.

### C.4 8-K trigger inventory

For ongoing-reporting issuers, 8-K disclosure items have specific triggering events and timing `[verify current SEC rule version]`. The skill records each contemplated or known triggering event and the date.

**Trigger categories to inventory:**
- Material definitive agreements (entry, amendment, termination).
- Bankruptcy or receivership.
- Material acquisitions and dispositions.
- Results of operations and financial condition.
- Creation of a material direct financial obligation; material off-balance-sheet arrangements.
- Triggering events that accelerate or increase a direct financial obligation.
- Costs associated with exit or disposal activities.
- Material impairments.
- Listing and delisting events; failures to satisfy listing standards.
- Unregistered sales of equity securities.
- Material modifications to rights of security holders.
- Changes in registrant's certifying accountant.
- Non-reliance on previously issued financial statements (restatement triggers).
- Changes in control.
- Departure / appointment of directors and Section 16 officers; material compensation plan actions.
- Amendments to articles, bylaws, or fiscal year.
- Temporary suspension of trading under employee benefit plans.
- Cybersecurity incidents (current SEC architecture) `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Other events the registrant elects to disclose under the catch-all item.
- Regulation FD disclosures.

### C.5 Proxy-statement specific items

**What to check:**
- Executive-compensation disclosure consistency (Summary Compensation Table figures, pay-versus-performance, perquisites).
- Director-independence and committee-composition disclosure.
- Related-party transactions.
- Beneficial-ownership table.
- Proposal-specific disclosure (auditor ratification, say-on-pay, equity plan adoption, charter amendments, M&A votes).
- Shareholder-proposal eligibility and response.

### C.6 Item-by-item checklist for an integrated review

The full item-by-item cross-reference matrix depends on the filings in scope. The attorney runs the materiality analysis; the skill builds the comparison and flags the discrepancies.

## D. Insider-Trading and Section 16 Framework

A framework for reviewing insider-trading policies, Rule 10b5-1 plan provisions, and Section 16 reporting facts. The skill records framework elements and gaps; the attorney concludes adequacy and compliance.

### D.1 Rule 10b5-1 plan elements

Rule 10b5-1 has been amended substantively, including with respect to cooling-off periods, plan-amendment treatment, single-trade plans, and required disclosures `[verify current SEC rule version at time of review]`.

**Facts to record from a plan:**
- The plan's adoption date and adoption circumstances.
- The cooling-off period applied (operative cooling-off period as of plan adoption) `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Whether the plan covers a single trade or a series of trades.
- The pricing and volume formula (specified prices and amounts; or a written formula).
- The grantor's representations regarding lack of MNPI at adoption.
- Plan-amendment and termination posture and the corresponding cooling-off-period implications.
- Required disclosures in periodic filings `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Whether the plan grantor has overlapping plans or recently terminated plans (which can affect availability of the affirmative defense).

### D.2 Insider-trading policy elements

**Facts to record from a policy:**
- Covered-persons scope (officers, directors, employees, contractors, family members, controlled entities, household members).
- MNPI definition (capturing both the materiality and the non-public elements; examples included).
- Trading-window architecture (open-window/closed-window mechanics tied to fiscal periods and earnings releases).
- Blackout-period architecture (event-driven blackouts; pension-plan blackouts under Reg BTR; ERISA-blackout overlap).
- Pre-clearance procedures (who must seek pre-clearance, who grants it, recordkeeping).
- 10b5-1 plan procedures (whether the policy requires 10b5-1 plans for executive trades; review and approval mechanics).
- Restrictions on gifts, pledges, hedging, derivatives, margin accounts, short sales, standing/limit orders.
- Restricted-list and watch-list mechanics (if the issuer operates a securities-law-relevant restricted list).
- Training, certification, and acknowledgment requirements.
- Escalation and enforcement (consequences for violations; reporting up).
- Recordkeeping and retention.

### D.3 MNPI inventory framework

When the skill is reviewing MNPI handling rather than the policy text:

- The categories of information the issuer treats as potentially MNPI (financial results, M&A activity, material litigation, regulatory inquiries, major customer/supplier developments, cyber incidents, management changes, capital-markets activity).
- The communication channels through which each category flows.
- The controls applied (need-to-know, deal codenames, restricted-list addition, blackout institution).
- The tipping-chain analysis structure: source → tippee → tippee's tippees; personal benefit test `[verify current SEC framework and applicable judicial standards]`.

### D.4 Section 16 reporting framework

Section 16 reporting obligations apply to officers, directors, and 10%+ beneficial owners of an issuer with a class of equity securities registered under Section 12 of the Exchange Act.

**Facts to record:**
- The universe of "Section 16 insiders" — directors, officers (including any designated as Section 16 officers under the Section 16 officer-designation framework), and beneficial owners of more than 10% of any class of registered equity securities.
- For each insider: title/role, date of becoming a Section 16 insider, date of any change in status.
- For each transaction: date, instrument, transaction code, amount, price, post-transaction holdings.
- The forms triggered (Form 3 on becoming an insider, Form 4 on changes in beneficial ownership, Form 5 for year-end residual transactions) `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- Whether any transaction has triggered the Section 16(b) short-swing-profit framework (the skill records the dates and the pairs; the attorney runs the §16(b) analysis).

**The skill never computes a Section 16 reporting deadline. All triggering dates are surfaced as `[deadline verification required]` facts.**

### D.5 Insider-trading-policy gap matrix

Build a matrix mapping each element above (D.2 and D.4) to whether the policy addresses it, partially addresses it, or omits it. Flag policy provisions that name a specific SEC rule version with a `[verify current SEC rule version]` reminder — rule version drift is a common gap.

## E. Form D and Blue-Sky Tracking Framework

A framework for organizing the Form D and state-notice-filing universe after a Reg D triggering fact. The skill builds the tracker; the attorney makes filing decisions and computes deadlines.

### E.1 Form D trigger and timing

The Form D filing is triggered by the first sale in a Reg D offering `[verify current SEC rule version]`. The skill records the triggering-fact date as a `[deadline verification required]` fact; the attorney computes the filing deadline.

**Facts to record:**
- The exemption claimed (506(b), 506(c), or another Reg D subsection).
- The triggering fact (typically first sale) and date `[deadline verification required]`.
- The issuer's Form D filer history (prior offerings, prior Form D filings, amendments).
- Form D content fields that depend on the offering: issuer identification, related persons, industry group, issuer size, offering size, dates, type of securities offered, business combination, minimum investment, sales commissions, finders' fees, use of proceeds `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

### E.2 Amendment triggers

Form D requires amendment in specified circumstances `[verify current SEC rule version]`. Common amendment triggers include: a material mistake of fact or change in information, an annual amendment if the offering continues for more than a year, a change in offering size beyond specified parameters.

**Facts to record:**
- Each event since the most recent Form D that could constitute an amendment trigger.
- The date of each event `[deadline verification required]`.

### E.3 State notice-filing universe

For each state in which a sale will occur, the state may require a notice filing of the Form D, a consent to service of process, and a fee. The notice-filing universe varies; the skill builds the state-by-state matrix.

**Per-state facts to record:**
- Whether a sale will occur (or has occurred) to a resident of the state.
- The state's notice-filing posture for the claimed federal exemption (most states require a Form D copy for 506 offerings notwithstanding NSMIA preemption of substantive regulation).
- Fee amount.
- Whether the state requires Form U-2 (Uniform Consent to Service of Process) or a state-specific equivalent.
- Whether the state participates in the NASAA Electronic Filing Depository (EFD) and whether the issuer files through EFD or directly.
- The state's amendment triggers and renewal mechanics.

**The skill never computes a state filing deadline.** Each triggering-fact date is a `[deadline verification required]` fact.

### E.4 EDGAR and coordinated-review mechanics

Form D is filed on EDGAR. The skill records the EDGAR CIK status, the filing agent (if any), and the EDGAR filing posture. For state filings, the NASAA EFD posture is recorded separately.

### E.5 Closing-loop with the underlying exemption

After the Form D and state filings are in the tracker, the skill closes the loop with the underlying exemption claim recorded in Section A — the exemption claimed in the Form D must match the exemption the issuer is relying on for the offering. Mismatch is a flag.

## F. Beneficial Ownership Framework (Schedule 13D / 13G)

A framework for analyzing Schedule 13D vs. 13G eligibility and the associated mechanics. The skill records facts; the attorney concludes eligibility and any reporting obligation.

### F.1 5% threshold trigger

The Schedule 13D/13G reporting regime is triggered when a person becomes the beneficial owner of more than 5% of a class of equity securities registered under Section 12 of the Exchange Act `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

**Facts to record:**
- The class of equity securities at issue and its outstanding share count.
- The reporting person's holdings, including shares the reporting person has the right to acquire within the period the current rule specifies `[verify current SEC rule version]`.
- The triggering-fact date (date of crossing the threshold) `[deadline verification required]`.

### F.2 Schedule 13D vs. 13G eligibility

**Facts to record:**
- Whether the reporting person fits within an eligibility category for short-form 13G reporting — qualified institutional investors, passive investors (subject to limits on holdings and intent), exempt investors `[verify current SEC rule version including any short-form-eligibility amendments]`.
- The reporting person's investment intent: passive holding only, or any acquisition with the purpose or effect of changing or influencing control.
- Whether the reporting person engages, intends to engage, or has the option to engage in any of the listed activities that disqualify 13G eligibility.

### F.3 Group formation

A "group" can form by agreement (oral or written) among two or more persons to acquire, hold, vote, or dispose of equity securities `[verify current SEC rule version including any group-formation guidance amendments]`.

**Facts to record:**
- Each person in the contemplated group, the relationship, and the basis for inferring agreement (formal agreement, course of conduct, communications, parallel actions).
- The holdings of each person individually and in aggregate.
- Whether the group's aggregate holdings cross the 5% threshold.

### F.4 Definition of "beneficial ownership"

Beneficial ownership for Section 13 purposes captures voting power and investment power, plus shares the person has the right to acquire within the period the current rule specifies `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

**Facts to record:**
- Direct shareholdings.
- Shares held through entities the reporting person controls.
- Options, warrants, convertibles, and other derivative securities and the expected timing of any conversion or exercise.
- Voting agreements, proxies, and arrangements that allocate voting or investment power.
- Cash-settled derivatives are treated per the current SEC framework `[verify current SEC rule version]`.

### F.5 Aggregation across reporting categories

Section 13 and Section 16 reporting both turn on beneficial ownership but use different thresholds (5% vs. 10%) and different filings. The skill records the underlying facts once and routes the analysis to both frameworks where applicable.

## G. Capital-Markets Closing Mechanics Framework

A framework for organizing the closing workstream for a public or private capital-markets transaction. The skill builds the tracker; the attorney closes the conditions.

### G.1 Underwriting / purchase / placement agreement structure

**Facts to record:**
- The agreement type (firm-commitment underwriting, best-efforts placement, private-placement subscription, debt purchase, registered direct, ATM, block trade, other).
- Closing conditions and bring-down points.
- Representations and warranties given by the issuer (and any selling stockholders) and the bring-down posture at closing.
- Indemnification structure and exclusions.
- Lock-up arrangements with directors, officers, and significant stockholders.
- Market-out / force-majeure provisions.

### G.2 Legal-opinion universe

The closing typically requires multiple opinions; the skill records each opinion contemplated and the facts each opinion needs.

**Opinions commonly required:**
- Issuer corporate / validity / authorization opinion.
- Exemption opinion (for private offerings) or no-registration opinion in resale contexts.
- 10b-5 disclosure letter (negative-assurance letter from issuer counsel — and underwriters' counsel — on the offering document; never a legal opinion on the document's accuracy in form, but a negative-assurance statement subject to specified review procedures) `[verify current accounting/auditing and SEC framework]`.
- Tax opinion (where required by the offering structure or by SEC rules).
- Foreign-counsel opinions where the issuer or selling stockholders include non-U.S. entities.

**For each opinion the skill records:** the addressee, the document being opined on, the assumptions and qualifications expected, and the diligence needed.

### G.3 Comfort letter and bring-down

The accountants' comfort letter provides agreed-upon procedures support for factual assertions in the offering document. The framework's accounting-standards reference is currently the AICPA's auditing standards (AT-C 215, formerly SAS 72) — applicable to offerings registered under the Securities Act `[verify current AICPA / SEC framework and any PCAOB equivalent]`.

**Facts to record:**
- The comfort-letter type contemplated (full SAS 72 / AT-C 215 comfort, limited-procedures letter, agreed-upon procedures letter, "circle-up" backup).
- Each factual assertion in the offering document that requires comfort or alternative backup (financial-statement-derived figures, percentages, ratios, share counts, operational metrics).
- The accountants' available comfort posture (which categories the accountants can comfort, which require alternative support).
- The pricing date and closing date — both as `[deadline verification required]` facts — at which comfort is provided and brought down.

### G.4 Officer and secretary certificates

**Facts to record:**
- The officer's certificate (representations and warranties bring-down at closing, no MAE, performance of covenants).
- The secretary's certificate (board resolutions, organizational documents, incumbency, good standing).
- Other certificates required by the agreement.

### G.5 Transfer agent, DTC, and legend mechanics

**Facts to record:**
- Transfer agent identity and instructions.
- DTC eligibility and processing posture.
- Legends required on the offered securities (Securities Act, Reg S, Rule 144, ERISA, blue-sky, contractual transfer restrictions).
- Funds-flow / settlement mechanics (DVP, escrow, paying agent, etc.).

### G.6 Regulatory and self-regulatory items

**Facts to record:**
- FINRA review status (for public offerings — corporate-financing-rule filings, underwriting-compensation review) `[verify current FINRA rule version]`.
- Exchange listing items (listing application, distribution requirements, additional-shares listing) `[verify current exchange rule version]`.
- Foreign-regulatory items where applicable.
- Blue-sky and Form D items (cross-reference Section E).

### G.7 Post-closing items

**Facts to record:**
- Post-closing Form D filing (cross-reference Section E).
- Post-closing 8-K or other periodic-filing triggers (cross-reference Section C).
- Greenshoe / over-allotment closing mechanics.
- Lock-up release mechanics.
- Closing-binder assembly and distribution.

### G.8 Bring-down points

Capital-markets closings have multiple bring-down points (signing, pricing, closing, settlement, greenshoe closing). The skill records each contemplated bring-down and the documents brought down at each, never computing any timing.

## H. Cross-Cutting Disclaimer

This catalog organizes the practitioner-known frameworks for issue-spotting securities-law questions. It does not advise on any specific offering, document, transaction, or person. Every framework reference carries jurisdiction- and rule-version markers because securities-law rule architecture has been amended substantively in recent years and continues to evolve. The catalog is a review aid for use by skills producing draft work product for attorney review only.

When in doubt, surface the question as a fact for attorney verification. The skill never concludes; the attorney concludes.
